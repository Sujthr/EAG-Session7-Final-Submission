# Exploring the Limits of Transfer Learning with a Unified Text-to-Text Transformer (T5)

**Authors:** Colin Raffel, Noam Shazeer, Adam Roberts, Katherine Lee, Sharan Narang, Michael Matena, Yanqi Zhou, Wei Li, Peter J. Liu
**Institution:** Google Research
**Year:** 2019 (arXiv), 2020 (JMLR)

---

## Overview

T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer) represents one of the most comprehensive empirical studies of transfer learning in NLP. Rather than introducing a single novel architecture, the paper's primary contribution is a unified framework that reformulates every NLP task as a text-to-text problem — where both inputs and outputs are always strings of natural language. This approach allows a single model and training objective to be applied across translation, summarization, question answering, classification, and regression tasks without architectural changes.

## Key Ideas

The central insight of T5 is deceptively simple: if you can express the input and desired output as text, you can train a single model to handle it. Sentiment classification becomes "Is this review positive or negative?" with the model outputting the word "positive" or "negative." Translation becomes "translate English to German: [sentence]." Regression tasks like STS-B produce a numeric string like "3.8." This uniformity simplifies the training pipeline significantly and enables large-scale multi-task learning.

The paper also introduces the **C4 dataset** (Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus), a 750GB filtered subset of Common Crawl. The authors describe their full data cleaning pipeline in detail, including deduplication, filtering heuristic pages, and removing offensive content — making C4 one of the most carefully documented large-scale pretraining corpora at the time.

## Architecture

T5 uses a standard encoder-decoder Transformer architecture, deliberately choosing not to use a decoder-only or encoder-only design. The encoder processes the input text and the decoder autoregressively generates the output. The model uses relative position embeddings rather than absolute sinusoidal embeddings.

The authors systematically evaluate architectural choices including:
- **Encoder-decoder vs. decoder-only vs. prefix LM**: Encoder-decoder wins for most generation tasks.
- **Self-attention masking strategies**: Causal masking, prefix masking, and fully visible masking are compared.
- **Position embeddings**: Relative scalar biases (added to attention logits) are preferred.

Model sizes range from T5-Small (60M parameters) to T5-11B (11 billion parameters), enabling a thorough study of scaling behavior.

## Pretraining Objective

T5 uses a **span corruption** objective (also called masked span prediction), which is a generalization of BERT's masked language modeling. Rather than masking individual tokens, T5 masks contiguous spans of tokens and replaces each span with a single sentinel token. The model must predict all masked spans, concatenated with sentinel tokens, as the target sequence. This is more efficient than token-by-token prediction and captures longer-range dependencies.

The authors compare this against several alternatives — language modeling, deshuffling, and BERT-style MLM — and find span corruption to be most effective for downstream transfer.

## Fine-Tuning and Multi-Task Learning

T5 explores three fine-tuning regimes:
1. **Full fine-tuning**: All parameters updated.
2. **Adapter layers**: Small bottleneck layers added between frozen pretrained layers.
3. **Gradual unfreezing**: Layers thawed sequentially during fine-tuning.

The paper also investigates multi-task learning, where tasks are mixed during pretraining or fine-tuning. They find that mixing proportions matter significantly, and that a simple "examples proportional" mixing strategy works well.

## Results

T5-11B set state-of-the-art results on 18 of 24 tasks in the GLUE and SuperGLUE benchmarks at publication time, as well as CNN/DailyMail summarization, SQuAD, WMT translation, and several commonsense reasoning benchmarks. On SuperGLUE, T5-11B achieved a score of 88.9, surpassing human performance (89.8) at the time of writing (though the benchmark was soon revised).

The scaling experiments confirm a clear log-linear relationship between model size and performance, a finding that would become central to subsequent scaling law research.

## Significance

T5's significance extends beyond its benchmark numbers. The unified text-to-text framework became the dominant paradigm for instruction-tuned and multi-task models. It directly influenced Flan-T5 (instruction-tuned), UL2, and the design of many later encoder-decoder models. The C4 dataset became a standard pretraining resource. Critically, T5's thorough ablation methodology set a standard for rigorous empirical NLP research, demonstrating that many common design choices (e.g., layer normalization placement, positional embeddings) have measurable and consistent effects. The paper remains a foundational reference for anyone studying transfer learning, multi-task learning, or the architecture of sequence-to-sequence models.
